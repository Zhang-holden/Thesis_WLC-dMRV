# ABSTRACT

The construction industry is one of the largest contributors to greenhouse gas emissions, requiring urgent reduction efforts. In particular, scope 3, which refers to indirect emissions from external activities such as material production, transportation, and subcontracted activities, remains insufficiently managed due to complex stakeholder networks and fragmented data. This study develops a blockchain-based life cycle assessment platform (BC-LCAP) for real-time tracking and management of greenhouse gas emissions across the building material supply chain. BC-LCAP integrates a web application, an application programming interface, and a blockchain network, utilizing chaincode functions tailored to each stakeholder's role to automatically calculate and record emissions at the ordering, manufacturing, transportation, and construction stages. Benchmark tests demonstrated high efficiency (create: $98.7\%$ up to 150 TPS; query: $99.8\%$ at 250 TPS) and low latency (create: 0.15 s; query: 0.01 s), while scalability was validated with the processing of up to 20,000 transactions. In addition, in a case study of a precast concrete construction project, BC-LCAP successfully calculated greenhouse gas emissions in real time at each stage of the supply chain (manufacturing: $1.77\mathrm{E} + 06\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ ; transportation: $2.02\mathrm{E} + 04\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ ; construction: $3.95\mathrm{E} + 04\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ ). By systematically encompassing scope 3 emissions, the platform addresses a critical blind spot in conventional greenhouse gas management approaches and establishes a foundation for enhancing carbon reduction strategies in the construction sector. 

# 1. Introduction

Greenhouse gas (GHG) generated by human activities gives rise to global warming, and this problem results in climate change, posing serious environmental threats, including ecosystem disturbance and rising sea levels [1-3]. To address this global issue, the international community has implemented a series of climate agreements that define clear emission reduction targets and collaborative mechanisms. The Kyoto Protocol, adopted in 1997, legally bound developed countries to 

reduce their GHG emissions by an average of $5.2\%$ below 1990 levels during the 2008-2012 commitment period [4]. This was followed by the Paris Agreement in 2015, under which 196 parties pledged to limit global warming to well below $2^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , with efforts to keep it below $1.5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ [5]. Most recently, the Glasgow Climate Pact in 2021 reaffirmed this ambition, with over 140 countries committing to net-zero targets, and approximately $70\%$ of global GDP now covered by such pledges. Nevertheless, global GHG emissions, which saw a temporary decrease due to the COVID-19 pandemic, rose again in 2022, hitting a record high

at $57.4\mathrm{GtCO_{2e}}$ (gigatons of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ equivalent). This figure represents a 1.2 $\%$ increase compared to 2021. Therefore, achieving carbon neutrality requires more realistic and effective reduction efforts [6]. 

To reduce national GHG emissions, it is important to manage emissions from major sectors such as energy, transportation, manufacturing, and construction. Among these, the construction industry plays a significant role, accounting for approximately $37\%$ of global GHG emissions as of 2023, making it one of the highest-emitting sectors globally [7]. Thus, to achieve the carbon neutrality goal, it is essential for the construction industry to reduce GHG emissions. In particular, for the effective control of the GHG emissions, it is important to adopt the real-time monitoring of GHG emissions based on the GHG Protocol, an international standardization methodology [8]. The GHG Protocol divides GHG emissions from companies into three categories: direct emission (scope 1) and indirect emission (scope 2 and scope 3) [9]. Scope 1 represents direct emission from construction sites and includes GHG emissions resulting from the fuel consumption of construction equipment and vehicles. Scope 2 represents indirect emissions generated in the process of producing the electricity or heat used for construction activities. Scope 3 represents all indirect emissions which are not controlled directly by the contractor but generated across the value chain building materials, including production, transportation, and disposal [10]. 

Previous studies on GHG emissions in the construction industry were focused primarily on scope 1 and scope 2 emissions, paying relatively less attention to the importance of the management of scope 3 emissions [11]. The lack of collaboration among stakeholders, complex supply chain structure, and absence of standardized data collection methods are among the many explanations available; however, the main one is that scope 3 emissions in the construction sector pose greater difficulty in terms of the management of relevant data, including data collection and assessment [10]. In other words, collecting and analyzing data for scope 1 and 2 emissions is easier because they arise from emission sources directly owned or controlled by companies. In contrast, scope 3 emissions arise from the value chain of companies. This makes it not only more difficult to collect and analyze data for scope 3 emissions, but also challenging to determine the scope and definition of scope 3 emissions [11]. Under these circumstances, recent studies stress the importance of scope 3 emissions. Sholi et al. [8], for example, presented the results of analysis that showed scope 3 emissions occurring in the supply chain of a construction project accounted for approximately $98\%$ of total GHG emissions. This means that managing GHG emissions not only from construction sites but also from the supply chain of building materials is vital for GHG reduction in a construction project. 

Across the building material supply chain including the manufacturing, transportation, and construction phases, a construction project involves diverse stakeholders such as owners, manufacturers, logistics and constructors. Due to the fragmented nature of the construction supply chain and the involvement of multiple actors, the vast amount of information generated throughout the building material supply process tends to be distributed and poorly integrated. This fragmentation of information presents a major challenge in conducting a life cycle assessment (LCA), which is a quantitative method for evaluating the environmental performance of building materials. Data related to material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, and construction are essential for conducting an accurate LCA. However, the current construction industry faces significant limitations in systematically collecting and managing such data. 

To address this issue, several IT-based GHG emission monitoring systems have been developed to capture and streamline carbon emissions across the construction supply chain [12,13]. However, these systems are typically centralized, which can limit transparency, trust, and data integrity among multiple stakeholders. In the construction industry, emission data is generated and managed by various parties—such as material suppliers, transporters, contractors, and site operators—using heterogeneous formats and platforms. While conventional 

IT systems can automate data aggregation, they often rely on a single managing entity, creating concerns about data manipulation, unequal access, and accountability. 

According to ReliaQuest's 2023 cyber threat report, the construction industry records the highest number of cyberattacks among all sectors, with an annual average of 226 incidents [14,15]. Owing to its heavy reliance on digital tools such as building information modeling (BIM), the internet of things (IoT), and cloud-based collaboration systems, incidents of project data breaches and ransomware attacks resulting in financial losses in the hundreds of millions of dollars have been consistently reported [16]. Notably, certain carbon calculation platforms collect sensitive corporate information, and prior analyses have indicated that these systems are susceptible to cybersecurity threats [17]. The leakage or manipulation of carbon emissions data can undermine the credibility of environmental reporting, result in regulatory non-compliance and associated legal penalties, damage corporate reputation and environmental, social, and governance ratings, and distort emission reduction strategies, thereby impeding genuine sustainability efforts. 

Based on these limitations, this study poses the following research questions: 

- Q1. Can blockchain be effectively integrated with LCA to enable real-time calculation and recording of GHG emissions across material supply chain stages? 

- Q2. Can smart contracts be designed to automate GHG emissions tracking in a way that aligns with national LCA guidelines and supply chain workflows? 

- Q3. Can a blockchain-integrated LCA platform improve the consistency, auditability, and scalability of GHG emissions data management across multiple stakeholders? 

To address these research questions, this study proposes the Blockchain-based life cycle assessment platform (BC-LCAP), a system that integrates blockchain technology with LCA to enable real-time, secure tracking of GHG emissions in the construction sector. 

First, the BC-LCAP captures key emission-related data across ordering, manufacturing, transportation, and construction stages through API-integrated web interfaces connected to each stakeholder. Second, smart contracts automatically calculate GHG emissions based on national LCA databases and record the results immutably on a blockchain ledger, minimizing human intervention and ensuring data integrity. Third, the platform structure allows for interoperability with existing systems and can be extended to support regulatory compliance, carbon credit mechanisms, and future integration with BIM for full life cycle emission monitoring.

# 3. Materials and method

To effectively manage GHG emissions produced by the construction industry and minimize their environmental impact, this study aims to develop a BC-LCAP which can integrate and manage information that is fragmented and generated throughout the building material supply chain. 

# 3.1. Development of the blockchain-based life cycle assessment platform

# 3.1.1. Architecture of the BC-LCAP

To address the complexity of managing GHG emissions data in the building material supply chain—where multiple stakeholders generate heterogeneous data at different stages and where ensuring data security, integrity, and confidentiality is critical—the BC-LCAP was designed with a three-layer architecture (i.e., application layer, API layer, and blockchain layer), considering user interaction, data exchange, and immutable data storage. Fig. 1 shows the overall architecture of the BC-LCAP. First, Application layer provides a client-centric interface that allows stakeholders of the building material supply chain to interact with the blockchain network. For the owner, for example, an owner-only dashboard where order-related information can be entered, and a form where input order information can be entered (e.g., price, quantity, manufacturer, etc.) were both developed. Additionally, through digital signature-based authentication and access control mechanisms, the application layer was configured to ensure the integrity and confidentiality and data and grant system access to authorized stakeholders only. In addition, validation functions were embedded to verify input data before transmission to the blockchain, preventing erroneous or fraudulent records. In this study, acceptable ranges were defined for each field to filter out abnormal values—for example, negative or implausibly large transportation distances trigger an error, while fuel use and material weight are limited to project-appropriate thresholds. This prevents manipulation or mistakes and ensures blockchain data reflects real site conditions, consistent with prior studies highlighting plausibility checks for reliable records [38,39]. Second, the API layer functions as middleware, enabling seamless and standardized communication between the application and blockchain layers. It transmits transaction proposals generated at the application level to the blockchain network—via interfaces such as REST APIs—and retrieves stored blockchain data for use by the application. By optimizing request-response handling according to blockchain network characteristics, the API layer enhances system scalability and performance, supporting the efficient processing of GHG emission data across the supply chain. In addition, it interfaces with the blockchain through the Peer command line interface (CLI), which allows direct execution of chaincode, submission of transactions, and secure retrieval of stored records. This architectural separation ensures that application-level operations remain insulated from the complexities of blockchain-level processes, thereby improving modularity and maintainability. 

Third, the blockchain layer is implemented on the hyperledger fabric consortium blockchain framework, selected for its suitability in multi-organizational supply chain contexts. Hyperledger fabric's permissioned network structure enforces participant identity verification, thereby ensuring trust, accountability, and compliance with data integrity and confidentiality requirements. Its modular architecture supports flexible chaincode deployment and fine-grained data management, enabling precise recording and traceability of GHG emissions generated throughout the manufacturing, transportation, and construction stages. Moreover, its high throughput and low-latency transaction processing capabilities make it well suited for real-time, high-volume data environments typical of complex supply chains. This layer forms the immutable, tamper-resistant foundation of BC-LCAP, ensuring 

the reliability of environmental data for both operational decision-making and regulatory compliance. 

Meanwhile, in the blockchain layer, two types of tasks (i.e., create and query) are performed in the chaincode according to the stakeholders involved in the construction project. The 'create' refers to the task of adding data to the blockchain network or modifying existing data. The 'query' refers to the task of searching and returning the data recorded on the blockchain network without changing the state of the data. Fig. 2 represents the data sequence diagrams of BC-LCAP according to 'create'. The 'Create' process in BC-LCAP begins with stakeholder-specific data entry through the application layer, where owners, manufacturers, logistics providers, and constructors submit operational information such as order details, manufacturing parameters, transportation logs, and construction activities. This ensures that GHG emissions are assessed using comprehensive, role-specific datasets (i). Once the information is submitted, the application layer validates the completeness and format of the data before issuing a 'Create' transaction request (ii). This request is securely transmitted by the API layer to the blockchain network using predefined communication protocols (iii). Within the blockchain layer, the hyperledger fabric SDK invokes the relevant chaincode function, which applies standardized emission calculation logic to ensure methodological consistency across stakeholders and project phases (iv). After execution, the transaction undergoes endorsement to confirm validity (v), and approved transactions are committed to the distributed ledger following consensus among peer nodes (vi). Once committed, the data becomes an immutable part of the blockchain (vii), and a block event is generated and relayed back through the API layer (viii). Finally, the application layer provides stakeholders with a confirmation that the data has been securely stored and is now verifiable for future audits or analyses (ix). Through this process, BC-LCAP establishes an immutable and verifiable record of GHG emission data, ensuring that all newly generated information is consistently validated, securely stored, and permanently traceable across the building material supply chain. 

Fig. 3 represents the data sequence diagrams of BC-LCAP according to 'query'. The 'Query' process starts when a stakeholder specifies retrieval parameters—such as order ID, material type, or date range—through the application layer, which structures the input into a standardized request format (i). The application layer prepares the 'Query' request to target the relevant blockchain records (ii) and securely transmits it via the API layer to the blockchain network (iii). Within the blockchain layer, the designated chaincode query function is invoked through the hyperledger fabric SDK, accessing the world state database, applying filters, and compiling the matching records into a structured output (iv). The results, typically in JSON format with timestamps, emission values, and metadata, are returned to the API layer without modifying the blockchain state (v). Finally, the API layer delivers the results to the application, which presents them through stakeholders-specific dashboards, ensuring timely and reliable access to verified environmental performance data (vi). Through this process, BC-LCAP enables stakeholders to efficiently retrieve and examine historical GHG emission records, providing trustworthy insights for compliance reporting, performance evaluation, and informed decision-making.

Of the six phases in the supply chain, the manufacturing phase will be taken as an example to explain the chaincode function (i.e., async function Manufacturing (type, data)) developed by this study (refer to Fig. 5). This function is executed when parameters related to manufacturing (e.g., price, distance, work hours, etc.) are entered. It can securely store and manage the GHG emissions occurring in the manufacturing process on the blockchain network. The '① Create ClientIdentity object to obtain user information') phase identifies the digital identity of the stakeholder (i.e., new ClientIdentity(ctx.stub)), and retrieves the attribute information where the role of the stakeholder is recorded (i.e., cidAttributeValue('role')). This procedure serves as the entry point for role-based logic implementation, which is a core component of the proposed permissioned blockchain architecture. It enforces access control policies by dynamically linking user identity to function-level permissions. This ensures that if the role of the stakeholder is 'manufacturer', the stakeholder is granted the permission to invoke the 'manufacturing' function of the chaincode on the blockchain network. If the role of the stakeholder is not 'manufacturer', invoking the 'manufacturing' function returns an error message (i.e., ② If the user isn't manufacturer, return an error). The data generated through these transactions is stored on-chain in hyperledger fabric using a key-value storage format, where the order ID serves as the key and the serialized JSON object of the order information is the value. # 3.2.2. Life cycle inventory analysis

To evaluate the environmental impacts caused by GHG emissions in the building material supply chain, it is necessary to conduct a life cycle inventory (LCI) analysis. This involves quantifying all substances inputted and emitted at each phase of the supply chain, including manufacturing, transportation, and construction. Two primary methods are commonly used to calculate environmental impacts: process-based LCA and input-output (I-O) LCA. The process-based method analyzes

environmental impacts by decomposing the production process into unit operations, using either directly measured or surveyed data on resource consumption and emissions. In contrast, the I-O LCA estimates overall environmental burdens, including indirect effects, based on national input-output tables. In this study, a hybrid LCA method is employed to combine the strengths of both approaches. A key assumption of this integration is that the I-O LCA provides comprehensive coverage of upstream and indirect emissions, while the process-based LCA offers more precise and detailed analysis at the operational level. By integrating both methods, the hybrid LCA improves the accuracy and completeness of GHG emissions estimation across the building material supply chain. Specifically, the I-O LCA is first used to estimate the total emissions both direct and indirect at each phase, using the input-output table published by the Bank of Korea [49]. These results are then used as the basis for the process-based LCA, which refines the estimates by applying more granular emission factors and process-specific data to calculate the final environmental impact substances. 

The emissions directly generated in the process of manufacturing building materials (i.e., $\mathrm{E}_{\mathrm{dir.}}^{\mathrm{M}}$ ) are calculated as shown in Eq.(1). The direct emissions from the manufacturing of building materials are calculated by multiplying the emissions directly generated from the combustion and production of the energy source by the energy use coefficient and material cost. The energy use coefficient represents the amount of energy directly used for producing one monetary unit of building materials. The indirect emissions from the manufacturing of building materials (i.e., $\mathrm{E}_{\mathrm{indir.}}^{\mathrm{M}}$ ) are calculated by multiplying the emissions indirectly generated from the combustion and production of the energy source by the energy use coefficient, the Leontief invers matrix, and the material cost (refer to Eq. (2)). The Leontief invers matrix, production inducement coefficients, represents the degree of direct or 

indirect change in all the related industrial sectors that occurs when a specific industrial sector changes by 1 unit. Namely, production inducement coefficients enable the calculation of indirect energy sources used by various industries in the building material manufacturing phase. 

$$
E ^ {M} _ {\text {d i r .}} = \left(M E C + M E P\right) \times E U C \times M C \tag {1}
$$

$$
E ^ {M} _ {\text {i n d i r .}} = \left(M E C + M E P\right) \times \left(E U C \times (I - A) ^ {- 1} \times M C\right) \tag {2}
$$

where, $E_{dir}^{M}$ is the direct emissions from manufacturing of building materials; MEC is the matrix whose element $mec_{il}$ represents the emission $i$ generated directly from the combustion of one unit of energy source $l$ ; MEP is the matrix whose element $mep_{il}$ represents the emission $i$ generated directly from the production of one unit of energy source $l$ ; EUC is the energy use coefficient representing the amount of energy used; $E_{indir}^{M}$ is the indirect emissions from manufacturing of building materials; MC is the material cost; $(I - A)^{-1}$ is the Leontief invers matrix. 

The direct emissions emitted by transportation vehicles and construction equipment in the process of the transportation and construction of building materials in the construction phase are calculated based on the amount of energy sources. Here, the amount of energy sources is calculated using Eq. (3) and Eq. (4). For example, the "P" apartment used 5364 tons of PC concrete. Using 5364 tons of PC concrete in Eq. (3), the amount of diesel directly used for transportation is calculated as 5149.44 liters $(= 2 \times 5.364$ tons $\times 30\mathrm{km} / (25\mathrm{tons}\times 2.5\mathrm{km} / 1))$ . Here, the load capacity and fuel efficiency of the trailer are 25 tons and 2.5 km/l, respectively [50]. The transportation distance was set at $30\mathrm{km}$ , as utilized in the development of the life cycle inventory database in South Korea [51]. Additionally, by applying 1071 units of PC concrete to Eq. 

(4), the amount of diesel directly used by the crane is calculated to be 5042.4 liters $(= 528\,h \times 19.1\,l/h / 2$ units/hour). Here, the crane's energy consumption per hour and work capacity are $19.1\,l/h$ and $1$ unit/hour, respectively [52]. Using Eq. (5) and (6), the amount of energy sources calculated by Eq. (3) and Eq. (4) translates into the direct and indirect emissions from transportation and construction of building materials. 

$$
e s ^ {\text {d i r} - T _ {l}} = 2 \sum_ {m = 1} \sum_ {j = 1} \frac {Q M _ {j}}{L C _ {j m}} \times \frac {T D _ {j m}}{E E _ {m l}} \tag {3}
$$

$$
e s ^ {d i r _ {-} C _ {l}} = \sum_ {n = 1} \sum_ {j = 1} \frac {Q M _ {j} \times E C _ {n l}}{W C _ {j n}} \tag {4}
$$

E ^ {T \& C} _ {\text {d i r .}} = \left(M E C + M E P\right) \times \left(e u ^ {\text {d i r －} T} _ {l} + e u ^ {\text {d i r －} C} _ {l}\right) \tag {5}
$$

$$

$$

where, $es_l^{dir\_T}$ is the physical amount of energy source $l$ used directly by a transportation vehicle; $QM_j$ is the quantity of material $j$ ; $TD_{jm}$ is the transportation distance of the vehicle $m$ loading material $j$ ; $LC_{jm}$ is the load capacity of vehicle $m$ for loading material $j$ ; $EE_{ml}$ is the energy efficiency of vehicle $m$ using energy source $l$ ; $es_l^{dir\_C}$ is the physical amount of energy source $l$ used directly by construction equipment; $EC_{nl}$ is the energy consumption of construction equipment $n$ using energy source $l$ per hour; $WC_{jn}$ is the work capacity of construction equipment $n$ representing the amount of work done per hour; $E_{dir}^{T\& C}$ is the direct emissions from transportation and construction of building materials; $E_{indir}^{T\& C}$ is the indirect emissions from transportation and construction of building materials; and $UP_l$ is the unit price of energy source $l$ . 

# 3.2.3. Life cycle assessment

Various substances calculated through the life cycle inventory analysis need to be converted into their environmental impact. This process typically consists of four steps (i.e., classification, characterization, normalization, and weighting). This study evaluates the environmental impact of GHGs from the supply chain of building materials, focusing on the stages of classification and characterization. Classification involves categorizing the identified substances from a life cycle inventory analysis into different environmental impact categories. 

Characterization quantifies the effects of these substances on their respective environmental categories. Here, the characterization factor for each substance is multiplied by the emissions calculated through inventory analysis to calculate the characterized environmental impacts (refer to Eq. (7)). 

$$
E I = E \times C F \tag {7}
$$

where, $EI$ is the characterized environmental impacts; $E$ is the emissions calculated through life cycle inventory analysis; and CF is the characterization factor. 

Meanwhile, when stakeholder data are entered into the BC-LCAP, the application transfers them via API to the blockchain network, where transaction processing and LCA calculation occur. The results are recorded on the ledger and returned through the API to the application, which then delivers them back to the stakeholder. Fig. 7 illustrates the blockchain-based transaction process integrated with the hybrid LCA applied in this study. When stakeholders input data such as type of material, quantity, and price, an API request is generated, which creates a transaction proposal within the blockchain. Subsequently, the chaincode is executed, invoking the LCA calculation through the chaincode invoke function. During this process, the emission factor—developed through a hybrid LCA that integrates both input-output LCA and process-based LCA—is utilized to analyze the environmental impacts, considering the classification and characterization stages. Namely, this study estimates the environmental impacts using emission factors derived from the hybrid LCA. Finally, the calculated environmental impacts are committed to the blockchain, and the API response is returned to the stakeholders.

# 4.2. Performance evaluation of the BC-LCAP

To verify the performance of the BC-LCAP developed in this study, a network benchmark was performed on the two functions that make up the blockchain network (i.e., create and query). The evaluation metrics for the network benchmark consist of three indices: transactions per second (TPS), latency, and throughput. The first index, TPS, represents the number of transactions that can be processed per second by the blockchain network [53,54]. This metric represents the overall processing capacity of the network and is used to evaluate the scalability of the system. The second index, latency, represents the time taken by the transaction submitted to the blockchain network to be recorded in the block [54]. This metric represents the responsiveness of the system and can be used to assess the feasibility of real-time data tracking and management. The third index, throughput, represents the number of transactions processed by the blockchain network [54,55]. This metric evaluates the actual performance of the system and is used to assess efficiency under real-world conditions, as opposed to TPS, a theoretical capacity. 

# 4.2.1. Network setup and test environment

The BC-LCAP was built based on the hyperledger fabric network. Smart contracts, referred to as "chaincode" in the fabric network, can be developed in various programming languages, including Go, JavaScript, and Java. This study developed chaincode based on JavaScript for its asynchronous processing ability. This chaincode can reduce latency by processing multiple tasks simultaneously. Moreover, this study used CouchDB, one of the two database types supported by hyperledger fabric (i.e., LevelDB and CouchDB). CouchDB is a NoSQL database in JSON format that can store values of various types, including texts, numbers, 

and lists. It supports complex rich queries. Thus, with the complexity of the supply chain of building materials taken into consideration, CouchDB was chosen as it provides complex query and data management capabilities. 

Meanwhile, to evaluate the performance of the BC-LCAP, this study used hyperledger capliper, an open-source project of the Linux foundation. Hyperledger caliper supports various blockchain platforms, including hyperledger fabric, ethereum, and corda. It is a benchmark tool capable of conducting a comparative analysis on various performance metrics, including send rate, throughput, and latency [55-57]. The test environment for evaluating the performance of the BC-LCAP was configured in a docker container based on the Ubuntu 22.04 LTS operating system. Additionally, the virtual machine used for the test was configured using the following hardware specifications: an Intel Core i7-10,700 processor offering 8-core and 16-thread processing capacity and 16GB of RAM. However, it should be noted that the actual performance may vary depending on system configuration and computing resources, as transaction throughput and latency can be affected by factors such as hardware specifications and network conditions. In addition, when scaled to enterprise-level deployments with more advanced infrastructure, the system performance can be further enhanced, offering higher processing capacity and improved stability.

# 4.2.4. Validation of network performance in BC-LCAP based on number of transactions

The txNumber represents the number of transactions that occur during the performance of a benchmark test. By measuring the changes in the send rate, throughput, and latency of the blockchain network that occur according to the changes in the txNumber, this study evaluates the maximum throughput and scalability of the blockchain network. To this end, while the number of transactions normally considered is from 1000 to 10,000, this study has extended it to 20,000 for analysis. This is because, given the complex nature of the GHG emissions tracking system, it was necessary to evaluate processing performance for a large number of transactions. Therefore, hyperledger caliper was used to analyze the send rate, throughput, and latency for five different numbers of transactions: 1000, 5000, 10,000, 15,000, and 20,000 (refer to Table S4). Fig. 11 presents the performance results of the BC-LCAP system under varying transaction volumes. As shown in subfigure (a), both the query and create functions maintained consistent throughput levels near 100 TPS throughout all test conditions (query: 99.9-100.0 TPS; create: 100.0-100.3 TPS). Subfigure (b) shows that average latency remained stable, with the query function fixed at 0.01 s and the create function at 0.08 s across all transaction volumes. In subfigure (c), the maximum latency of the query function remained within 0.03-0.04 s, while the create function increased from 0.18 s to a peak of 0.18 s before slightly decreasing to 0.16 s. Subfigure (d) indicates that minimum latency was also constant: 0.01 s for query and 0.04 s for create. The results show that the BC-LCAP system can stably process a cumulative total of 20,000 transactions without any degradation in throughput or latency, indicating that it can be reliably applied to real-world construction supply chain environments where large volumes of transactions occur repeatedly.

# 5. Discussion

As mentioned in Section 1, this study aimed to verify three hypotheses regarding the feasibility of integrating blockchain with LCA to enable real-time tracking of GHG emissions in the construction material supply chain: (i) Q1: Can blockchain be effectively integrated with LCA to enable real-time calculation and recording of GHG emissions across material supply chain stages? (ii) Q2: Can smart contracts be designed to automate GHG emissions tracking in a way that aligns with national LCA guidelines and supply chain workflows? and (iii) Q3: Can a blockchain-integrated LCA platform improve the consistency, auditability, and scalability of GHG emissions data management across multiple stakeholders? 

- (i) BC-LCAP embeds GHG emissions calculation logic into the chaincode, enabling real-time tracking and recording of emissions during the manufacturing, transportation, and construction stages. When applied to a precast concrete construction project, the emissions were quantified as $1.77\mathrm{E} + 06\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ . for manufacturing, $2.02\mathrm{E} + 04\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ . for transportation, and $3.95\mathrm{E} + 04\mathrm{kg - CO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ . for construction, demonstrating BC-LCAP's capability for real-time GHG management across the entire process. Although this validation was conducted using precast concrete, BC-LCAP is implemented based on supply chain phases (i.e., manufacturing, transportation, and construction) involving multiple stakeholders, making it adaptable to various other construction materials such as steel, timber, and cement. 

- (ii) Unlike previous studies that considered only direct emissions, this study designed smart contracts in accordance with internationally recognized LCA guidelines (such as ISO 14040 and ISO 14044) to calculate not only direct but also indirect GHG emissions. A scenario-based analysis was conducted to compare with previous studies, and the results showed that BC-LCAP, by including indirect emissions, quantified a total of $1.83\mathrm{E} + 06\mathrm{kg - CO_{2eq}}$ , demonstrating its capability for more comprehensive and inclusive analysis. While the current implementation applies Korean regulatory and cost data, the framework can be adapted to various regional contexts by replacing the national LCA database and adjusting cost parameters. For example, the Ecoinvent and the United States environmental protection agency LCI databases provide validated life-cycle data on carbon emissions, energy consumption, and environmental impact indicators for construction materials. Integrating these international databases into BC-LCAP would further expand its applicability. 

- (iii) BC-LCAP provides traceable GHG emissions records to various stakeholders, including owners, manufacturers, logistics, and 

constructors, and its scalability and reliability were verified through performance benchmarking. Hyperledger caliper benchmark results showed that the create function maintained over $98.7\%$ efficiency with an average latency of 0.15 s at up to 150 TPS, while the query function achieved $99.8\%$ efficiency with an average latency of 0.01 s at 250 TPS. These performance results demonstrate the platform's capability to scale to large-scale platform without any degradation in performance. 

While the proposed BC-LCAP platform demonstrates technical feasibility in tracking and managing GHG emissions across the building material supply chain, it is also necessary to discuss alternative perspectives—specifically, the limitations of LCA-blockchain integration and the potential for BIM integration—to fully justify the current proposed solution. (i) Limitations of LCA-blockchain integration: The large volume of life-cycle data makes full on-chain storage inefficient, requiring a hybrid approach in which only critical, verified records are stored on-chain while the remainder is maintained off-chain [64]. The lack of standardized LCA data formats and input procedures may hinder interoperability and auditability. In addition, ensuring the reliability of input data in a decentralized environment remains a challenge, and adoption may be limited by factors such as digital literacy gaps, privacy and ownership concerns, and the absence of regulatory incentives. These limitations can be addressed by establishing standardized LCA data schemas and input protocols, implementing validation mechanisms such as third-party verification and automated consistency checks, and developing hybrid storage architectures that integrate blockchain with scalable off-chain databases. 

(ii) Potential for BIM integration: BIM is effective for managing 3D models and attribute information during the design and construction stages. However, a substantial portion of GHG emission data originates from external systems beyond BIM's scope, including manufacturing, transportation, and construction [65]. While integrating LCA data into BIM can support environmental performance analysis during these phases, it is less effective for ensuring transparent, supply-chain-wide tracking. In contrast, blockchain can comprehensively capture, manage, and verify life cycle and multi-stakeholder data, including information outside of BIM, and can optionally be linked to BIM to enhance visualization and analytical capabilities [66]. For these reasons, this study prioritizes blockchain as the primary framework to address the fragmentation, complexity, and traceability challenges of construction projects, while considering BIM integration as an optional extension. In the future, LCA data collected and verified via blockchain could be integrated with BIM for visualization, comparative analysis, and environmental performance optimization in the design and construction phases. 

In addition to demonstrating technical feasibility, it is also important to consider integration with other blockchain-based systems. Recent studies in construction have explored platforms such as e-inspection systems, which record quality and safety results on blockchain to enhance transparency and prevent tampering [67,68]. Similarly, the material passport approach has been proposed to document material attributes and histories on blockchain to enable the circular reuse of construction waste [69,70]. In addition, smart payment platforms have been developed to automate transactions among owners, contractors, 

and subcontractors through smart contracts [71,72]. However, if these systems develop in isolation, projects risk fragmented blockchains. Future platforms should therefore pursue integration with existing systems. First, data standardization: by defining emissions records, inspection results, material histories, and payment data in common formats such as IFC, COBie, or GS1/EPCIS, the platform can interoperate seamlessly with other systems within a project [73]. Second, cross-contract invocation: for example, once an inspection result is approved on site, the data can be automatically recorded in the emissions ledger and simultaneously linked as a payment condition in a smart payment contract [74-76]. In this way, quality control, emissions tracking, and financial settlement are integrated into a continuous and connected workflow rather than existing as separate processes. 

# 6. Conclusion

The construction industry is one of the largest contributors to GHG emissions, necessitating reductions more urgently than in any other sector. Reducing GHG emissions in construction requires a focused approach on the supply chain of building materials. However, systematic approaches to GHG emission management at the supply chain level remain lacking. To solve this problem, this study proposed the BC-LCAP, a platform leveraging the LCA of the supply chain of building materials and blockchain technology. By using the distributed ledger of blockchain and chaincode, the BC-LCAP attempted to overcome the limitations of the existing LCA performance system. Based on the hyperledger caliper benchmark tests, the create function achieved over $98.7\%$ efficiency at 150 TPS with an average latency of $0.15\mathrm{s}$ while the query function maintained $99.8\%$ efficiency at 250 TPS with a consistent latency of $0.01\mathrm{s}$ . Also, a precast concrete case study demonstrated that BC-LCAP calculated and recorded a total of approximately $1.83\mathrm{E} + 06\mathrm{kgCO}_{2\mathrm{eq}}$ across the manufacturing, transportation, and construction stages, confirming its practical applicability for real-time emission tracking and management. The BC-LCAP allows each stakeholder (i.e., owner, manufacturer, logistics, and constructor) to enter relevant information, such as order, manufacturing, transportation, and construction, via a web application. This enables real-time GHG emission calculations and ensures reliable and secure data management. 

The BC-LCAP developed in this study enables real-time monitoring of data, including GHG emissions, generated in the supply chain of building materials. This facilitates the potential for better and more accurate decision-making for stakeholders responsible for managing environment impacts. The BC-LCAP platform has strong potential to support carbon trading mechanisms and regulatory compliance frameworks. By ensuring transparent and tamper-proof recording of GHG emissions across the supply chain, the platform can provide verified emissions data for emission allowance trading or offset schemes. Moreover, the ability to track real-time emissions at each stage aligns with national and international reporting standards such as ISO 14,064 and the EU ETS, enhancing regulatory compliance. Given the global push toward decarbonization in the construction sector, BC-LCAP has the potential to serve not only as a foundational infrastructure for industry-wide GHG reduction efforts but also as an enabling platform for future carbon markets and compliance ecosystems. Looking ahead, the integration of forward-looking LCA approaches that incorporate insights from patented technologies and emerging innovations could further enhance BC-LCAP's capability. By identifying and assessing the environmental impacts of newly developed low-carbon materials and processes, the platform could proactively support the adoption of breakthrough solutions, strengthening its role in promoting sustainable construction practices and accelerating industry-wide decarbonization. In the case study, precast concrete was selected as the target material due to its integrated nature, encompassing production, transportation, and on-site installation within a single system. This characteristic makes it a reference for extending the BC-LCAP framework to other materials such as steel or timber. By substituting the material type, the same full-cycle 

structure can be applied, allowing for consistent and transferable environmental impact assessments across various construction materials.
